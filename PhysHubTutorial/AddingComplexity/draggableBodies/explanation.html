Inellastic collisions

This is the most common kind of collision. This is what you see when you let a very soft object hitting another object. Energy gets dissipated in many forms. This energy was kinetic energy of the colliding object.

Although very important, the problem with this kind of collision is that it is very difficult to predict collision behaviour when collisions are innelastic. The reason being that the exact ammount of energy that gets lost in the collision will vary widely with colliding material and other conditions such as pressure.

Before closing this section however, I want to reinforce one thing. So far, no human has ever observed any collision disobey the law of conservation of momentum. The reason? Nobody really knows. But if you respect conservation of momentum, and if your algebra is sharp, you will always make right predictions about velocities of objects before and after the collision. If you want to know more about conservation of momentum. Read the next section. It introduces ellastic collisions with conservation of momentum.

Ellastic collisions

Ellastic collisions are collisions where not only the momentum but also the total kinetic energy of the colliding bodies is conserved. That means that if two or more objects that collide ellastically are left without external influences, they will never speed down. Their speeds may vary with time. But the total kinetic energy and momentum of the system will be conserved.

This is a fundamental property of the universe that albeit not obeyed by large objects, is regorously obeyed by atoms. These have never been observed to collide innelastically. This sould ring a bell in your head right now!!! The universe is a bag of atoms. Which we know to collide ellastically, hence energy is conserved within the universe. But if the total energy content of the universe is fixed, where did the amount that is already here come from? Hopefully you also want to know the answer to this question. Every physicist does. To get closer to the answer, it may help to try and predict the behaviour of objects that collide ellastically like atoms.

The first concept that one needs to understand in this section is conservation of momentum. The law of conservation of momentum states that the sums of the products of mass and velocities of objects in a closed system shall not change. In leimans terms, if you leave a bunch of objects in a box in vacuum and no external forces like asteroids act on the box, the total momentum of the box and its contents will not change. The momentum being the sum of the velocities multiplied by the masses of each object inside and including the box.

Mathematically, momentum is described as $$P = mv$$, where \(P\) is the momentum, \(m\) the mass and \(v\) the velocity of the object.

Note however, that this definition indicates that the direction of the velocity matters. This is what makes velocity different than speed. Velocity contains information about the direction of movement. Now you should be asking yourself. If two people can interpret the same velocity differently because they are in different places (maybe they are looking at the same object from different) sides. How does one know what is the correct sign of the velocity?

Short answer, one does not have to. What one can do, and this works in practice, is take the velocities of both objects relative to one another or another object. If they are travelling in oposite directions, they have oposite signs for their velocities.

But before showing you how, I will write down the mathematical description of conservation of momentum. Which reads that the sum of momentums before a collision is equal to the sum of momentums after the collision. $$\sum{P_i} = \sum{P_f} $$

THis is valid for all directions simultaneously!!! This is the best part. Therefore, this can be written using vector notation: $$\sum{P_i} = \sum{P_f} $$

With this in mind, lets go back to the situation where we had two balls with equal masses and velocities. Lets give them the names \( b_1\) and \( b_2\), for the balls on the left and on the right respectively. \( b_2\) has a mass that is twice as high as that of \( b_1\), which has a mass \( m_1\). The speed before the collision is the same for both balls. If the heavier ball (\( b_2\)) loses half of its speed. How fast must the other ball be going after the collision?

Lets write what we know: $$\sum{P_i} = \sum{P_f} $$ $$\sum{P_i} = m_1v_1+m_2v_2$$ $$\sum{P_f} = m_1\frac{v_1}{2}+m_2v_?$$ $$v_1 = -v_2$$

Now you just have to use algebra to isolate the unknown term: v_? = \frac{m_1v_1+m_2v_2 - m_1\frac{v_1}{2}}{m_2} Which can be simplified by using: $$v_1 = -v_2$$ $$m_1 = -\frac{1}{2}m_2$$ I didnt write this on paper. So it coulr be wrong.

Now, what if we could find the velocities of BOTH colliding objects after the collision, knowing only their values before the collision? This can be done for ellastic collisions and this is the reason. If you have \(n\) variables you do not know, you need \(n\) hints about each varaible's identities to find out the value of each one. This translates into us needing \(n\) equations to find \(n\) variables in those equations. For ellastic collisions we have in general 2 equations: $$\sum{P_i} = \sum{P_f} $$ and $$\sum{K_i} = \sum{K_f} $$ Where \(K_i\) and \(K_f\) are the initial and final kinetic energies of the objects involved in the collision. With this in mind it might be obvious why ellastic collisions are so nice. But I will make it extra clear:

Since for every ellastic collision between two objects the two equations above are true, and since these formulas only have 4 variables, you only need two of these 4 to find the missing 2!!! This is extremely useful in some fields. Now, how do we put this in practice?

We just set up the equations. It is a very mathematical kind of problem. It really is just algebra. But lets take this a step further with the most common use to understanding ellastic collisions: Knowing the final velocities of two objects after they collide. So let us try to understand a problem like this. First we set up a very general case:

We have two balls which we designate the numbers 1 and 2 for. Furthermore, the subscripts \(i\) and \(f\) will be used to indicate initial/final conditions, hence before and after the collision respectively. Hence for the problem set up we know: $$ m_1, m_2, v_{1, i} and v_{2,i}$$ $$ P_i = P_f $$ $$ P_i = m_1v_{1, i}+ m_2v_{2, i}$$ $$ P_f = m_1v_{1, f}+ m_2v_{2, f}$$ $$ K_i = K_f $$ $$ K_i = frac{1}{2}m_1_{1, i}^2 + frac{1}{2}m_1_{2, i}^2 $$ $$ K_f = frac{1}{2}m_1_{1, f}^2 + frac{1}{2}m_1_{2, f}^2 $$ and we wish to find: $$v_{1, f} and v_{2,f}$$ After a LOT of algebra, you will find the following result: $$ v_{1, f} = \frac{m_1-m_2}{m_1+m_2}v_{1, i} - \frac{2m_2}{m_1+m_2}v_{2, i}$$ $$ v_{2, f} = \frac{m_2-m_1}{m_1+m_2}v_{2, i} - \frac{2m_1}{m_1+m_2}v_{1, i}$$

Now, you have a very cool set of equations that help you obtain the final speeds of balls that collide head on along some direction. What about balls travelling in different directions? It is very simple. Just get the part of the velocity that is going torwards each ball. This way you will still be getting a "head on" collision. Like in the animation below:

This can be done by making use of the dot product!!! But as much as I would like teaching this topic. I think 3blue1Brown already did it very well. Hence I recommend his video for learning the dot product. But to end this explanation section, I wish to explain how the dot product fits into this.

If both bodies have known velocities, these can be represented as vectors. If we represent both vectors in the same coordinate system, the component of this velocity can be readily calculated by multiplying the speed along the desired direction by the cosine of the angle between this desired direction and the velocity. Then, you will get a speed that is the part of your velocity that goes in the desired direction. This can be used in the formulas above, and the final velocities can again be broken down into components of your choosing. Usually, but not restricted to, along x, y, and z.
